Stroke Recognition

Why transient stroke symptoms must never be ignored

Transient neurological symptoms — sudden weakness on one side, slurred speech, vision changes, or brief dizziness — can be easy to dismiss, especially when they improve on their own within minutes or hours. But these episodes can represent a transient ischemic attack (TIA), which the American Stroke Association and the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke both consider a medical emergency requiring urgent evaluation.

There is no reliable way for a patient at home to distinguish a TIA from a stroke in the moment. Symptoms that resolve quickly can still indicate significant underlying cerebrovascular disease — including large artery stenosis, cardioembolic sources such as atrial fibrillation, or small vessel disease — all of which carry real risk of a completed stroke within hours to days if untreated.

  • Symptoms that disappear may still represent a warning sign of impending stroke.
  • There is no at-home test to reliably differentiate TIA from stroke.
  • Urgent evaluation is essential even when symptoms fully resolve.
  • Common mimics include seizure with post-ictal weakness, migraine aura, hypoglycemia, peripheral vertigo, and functional neurological symptoms.
  • Ruling out a mimic requires careful history, neurological exam, glucose check, vital signs, and often neuroimaging.

The public message remains simple: any sudden focal neurological symptom — even one that goes away — should be treated urgently until a trained medical team has evaluated the patient. When it comes to the brain, time is never a reason to wait and see.

Stroke Prevention

Blood pressure: the single most modifiable risk factor for stroke

If there is one number every adult should know, it is their blood pressure. Hypertension is the leading modifiable risk factor for both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke, responsible for a substantial proportion of strokes worldwide. Yet it is often called the “silent killer” because it causes no symptoms until damage has already been done — to the brain, the heart, and the blood vessels.

From a vascular neurology standpoint, uncontrolled hypertension causes injury through several mechanisms. Chronically elevated pressure damages the walls of small penetrating arteries deep in the brain, leading to lacunar infarcts and white matter disease. It also accelerates atherosclerosis in large arteries including the carotid arteries, aortic arch, and intracranial vessels. And it dramatically increases the risk of intracerebral hemorrhage — bleeding directly into the brain tissue — which carries a far higher mortality than most ischemic strokes.

The good news is that blood pressure is treatable, and even modest reductions in systolic pressure translate into meaningful reductions in stroke risk. Current guidelines from the American Heart Association and American Stroke Association target a blood pressure below 130/80 mmHg for most adults with established hypertension, particularly those with prior stroke or TIA.

  • Know your numbers — a blood pressure above 130/80 mmHg warrants attention and discussion with your physician.
  • Lifestyle changes including reduced sodium intake, regular aerobic exercise, weight management, and limiting alcohol all lower blood pressure meaningfully.
  • Medication adherence is critical — missing doses is a common and preventable cause of poorly controlled hypertension.
  • Home blood pressure monitoring can help track trends between clinic visits and improve long-term control.
  • For stroke survivors, blood pressure control is one of the most important steps in preventing a second stroke.

Stroke prevention is not only about what happens in the emergency department or the catheterization lab. Much of it happens in the choices people make every day — and understanding why blood pressure control matters is the first step toward making those choices count.
