What is a stroke?

A stroke happens when blood flow to the brain is blocked or when a blood vessel in the brain ruptures. Without enough oxygen, brain cells can be injured quickly, which is why urgent recognition and treatment are so important.

  • Ischemic stroke: caused by a blocked blood vessel
  • Hemorrhagic stroke: caused by bleeding in or around the brain
  • Transient ischemic attack (TIA): a warning event that still needs urgent medical attention

Know the warning signs: F.A.S.T.

Sudden symptoms should never be ignored. Use F.A.S.T. to recognize common stroke warning signs and seek emergency care immediately.

F

Face

Does one side of the face droop or feel numb? Ask the person to smile.

A

Arm

Is one arm weak or numb? Ask the person to raise both arms.

S

Speech

Is speech slurred or hard to understand? Ask them to repeat a simple sentence.

T

Time

Call 911 immediately. Time is critical and every minute matters.

Other sudden symptoms to watch for

Sudden weakness, numbness, trouble seeing, confusion, dizziness, loss of balance, or a severe headache can also be signs of stroke.

Prevention and healthy living

Many strokes can be prevented. A strong prevention strategy combines medical care, risk-factor control, and healthy daily habits.

Blood pressure control

High blood pressure is one of the most important stroke risk factors. Regular monitoring and treatment matter.

Heart and vessel health

Manage cholesterol, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, and other vascular risks with your clinical team.

Lifestyle steps

Exercise regularly, avoid smoking, limit alcohol, sleep well, and follow a heart-healthy diet.

Recovery and rehabilitation

Recovery after stroke is different for every person. Rehabilitation may involve physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language therapy, cognitive support, and emotional support.

Why rehab matters

Structured rehabilitation helps patients regain function, practice daily skills, and build independence over time. Repetition, timing, and individualized therapy plans can all influence recovery.

Family support

Family members and caregivers play a major role by encouraging therapy participation, helping monitor symptoms, and supporting recovery goals at home and in the community.

Recent stroke research highlights

This section offers simple summaries of current themes in stroke research for educational purposes. It is not a substitute for professional medical advice or formal literature review.

Emergency recognition and rapid treatment

Modern stroke systems continue to emphasize faster recognition, emergency transport, and rapid evaluation for treatments such as thrombolysis and thrombectomy.

Rehabilitation timing and intensity

Research continues to explore how therapy timing, repetition, strength training, and neuroplasticity-informed rehabilitation affect recovery.

Technology-supported recovery

Investigators are studying robotics, imaging, remote monitoring, and other technology-enabled approaches to support long-term stroke recovery.
